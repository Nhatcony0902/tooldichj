# Social / Marketing Post Playbook

For `t1k:human-writing` post register (LinkedIn, X, announcements, blog intros). Sources verified 200 (end).

## The AI post formula (avoid all five parts)

In a dataset of 500 AI LinkedIn posts, 82% followed this skeleton:

1. Single-line dramatic hook (contrarian claim / fake stat / manufactured vulnerability)
2. Pivot line — "Here's why:" / "Let me explain." / "Here's the thing:"
3. Numbered list or emoji bullets (3–7 one-sentence items)
4. Summary — "The takeaway?" / "Bottom line:" (restates the hook)
5. Engagement-bait closer — "Agree? 👇" / "What's your take?"

## Hook clichés (blocklist)

Appear at 19–34× normal frequency. Three+ in one post = near-certain AI:
- "Here's the thing" · "Let that sink in." · "Read that again." · "Game-changer"
- "Unpopular opinion:" · "I'll say it louder for the people in the back"
- "In a world where…" · "It's no secret that…" · "Let's face it…"
- "Here's the uncomfortable truth:" · "Now more than ever" · "Picture this." · "Ever wondered…?"

## Formatting tells
- **Broetry** — one coherent thought split into one-sentence paragraphs stacked vertically (gaming the "See more" click). 91% of AI posts used identical one-sentence-per-line spacing — the single highest-confidence structural tell.
- Emoji as bullets (✅ 🚀 💡) on every list item; none in the body.
- Every list item the same length; bold on every "key word" (so nothing stands out).
- Length lands exactly in the 700–1,300-char "viral sweet spot" — because it was prompted to, not because the thought needed it.

## Fake vulnerability tell
The ratio gives it away: emotional setup is specific-*sounding* but detail-free ("I was struggling with burnout"), while the lesson is suspiciously tidy ("Here are 5 frameworks I used"). Real vulnerability has messy specifics and no clean lesson arc.

## Engagement-bait closers (drop them)
"Agree? 👇" · "What's your take?" · "Drop your thoughts below." · "Tag someone who needs this." · "Save this for later." · "Which one resonates most?" — all answerable in one word, because they're not real questions.

## Human post playbook
1. **Specific lived detail, not vague narrative.** "We shipped a playable ad on Day 11 of a 14-day deadline. Client found a critical bug on Day 12. We fixed it in 4 hours by pulling two engineers off the next project. Cost us $3k. I'd do it again." The numbers are the tell — AI gives the *shape* of a story, humans give the content.
2. **An opinion with stakes.** "I stopped doing 1:1s for 6 months. Productivity went up. I don't think 1:1s work in teams under 8 people." Falsifiable = human.
3. **Imperfection and friction.** A tangent, a mid-list "I'm not sure this applies everywhere", one sentence that undercuts the main point. AI is frictionless hook→CTA.
4. **No formula.** A single flowing paragraph, or an unusual format — questions with no answers, a retelling with no lesson.
5. **Pub test.** Would you say this out loud in a meeting? "Leveraging transformative solutions is crucial" — no. "We tried three tools and hated all of them" — yes.
6. **Real numbers, dates, names** — they anchor the post in checkable reality.

## Before / after

**Before (AI — every tell present):**
> I failed 3 times before I found success.
> Here's what nobody tells you:
> ✅ Failure is just feedback.
> ✅ Every setback is a setup for a comeback.
> ✅ Your mindset determines your outcomes.
> The takeaway? Embrace the journey.
> What's your biggest lesson from failure? 👇

**After (human):**
> We launched our third game in 2023. It hit $0 in the first two months — not "disappointing", literally zero organic downloads after the marketing budget ran out.
> I spent about a week convincing myself it was a market-timing problem. It wasn't. We'd built a game that was technically fine but had no identity — nothing you could say in one sentence that someone would repeat to a friend.
> We shelved it and spent the next project obsessing over one question before writing any code: "What's the one sentence someone says when they recommend this?" Still not sure we got it right. But asking it changed what we built.
